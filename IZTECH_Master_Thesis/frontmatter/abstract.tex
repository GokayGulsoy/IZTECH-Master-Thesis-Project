\begin{abstractenv}
    The deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) via Machine Learning as a Service (MLaaS) platforms raises critical data privacy concerns, as users must transmit sensitive information to untrusted servers. Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE) enables computation directly on encrypted data, but adapting Transformer architectures to the encrypted domain requires replacing all non-polynomial operations with low-degree polynomial approximations \emph{and} eliminating---or drastically reducing---the dominant ciphertext-by-ciphertext multiplications that arise from data-dependent attention.

    This thesis presents two complementary contributions. First, the Learnable Polynomial Approximation Network (LPAN): a three-stage progressive framework that replaces \emph{all three} non-linear operations in BERT---GELU, Softmax, and LayerNorm---with learnable Chebyshev polynomial approximations, using distribution-weighted minimax initialisation, progressive layer-by-layer replacement with attention-level knowledge distillation, per-head polynomial Softmax, and co-adaptation for polynomial LayerNorm. Evaluated on four BERT variants on SST-2 and BERT-Base on MRPC, LPAN achieves accuracy drops of only 0.11--1.72\%---competitive with or exceeding MPCFormer (91.3\%) which replaces only two operations.

    Second, \textbf{Synthesizer-LPAN} (\emph{Synthesizer Learnable Polynomial Activation Network}): an architectural extension that replaces standard self-attention with a frozen, learned, plaintext attention pattern $A \in \mathbb{R}^{L\times L}$ in the spirit of Tay et al.\ (2020). Under FHE this eliminates the $W_q$ and $W_k$ projections, the $Q\!\cdot\!K^\top$ ciphertext-by-ciphertext multiplications, and the depth-12 polynomial Softmax \emph{entirely}. Combined with Halevi--Shoup baby-step giant-step (BSGS) fusion of the cyclic-shift mask with each plaintext diagonal, batched LPAN polynomial evaluation, and a tuned CKKS chain budget, Synthesizer-LPAN achieves a \emph{single-GPU sub-100-second end-to-end coherent FHE BERT result on plain HEonGPU}: \textbf{60.9\,s} for a 12-layer forward pass at sequence length $L{=}128$ on a single NVIDIA H100, a $\mathbf{13.67\times}$ speed-up over the honest LPAN baseline of $833$\,s. Concurrent Cerium results are faster on the same hardware via a compiler/runtime stack; our contribution is therefore positioned as a complementary architectural redesign rather than a current latency-SOTA claim.

\end{abstractenv}
